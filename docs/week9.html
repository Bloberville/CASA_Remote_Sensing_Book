8.1 Weekly Learning Diary

Summary

This week introduced the use of remote sensing for measuring surface temperature, focusing on how thermal data can be used to analyse environmental and urban conditions. Unlike previous weeks which focused on classification and accuracy, this topic explored how remotely sensed data can be used to derive physical properties of the Earth’s surface, specifically land surface temperature (LST).

A key concept was the use of thermal infrared sensors, such as those available on Landsat, which detect emitted radiation rather than reflected energy. This distinguishes thermal remote sensing from optical approaches, as it enables the measurement of surface temperature based on the intensity of emitted radiation. The process of deriving land surface temperature involved several steps, including converting digital numbers to radiance, then to brightness temperature, and applying corrections to account for emissivity.

The practical component focused on applying these steps to Landsat imagery to calculate land surface temperature and explore spatial patterns. This provided insight into how temperature varies across different land cover types, particularly within urban environments where materials such as concrete and asphalt retain heat differently to vegetation. This highlighted the concept of the urban heat island effect, where built-up areas tend to experience higher temperatures than surrounding rural areas.

However, there are important limitations to consider. Thermal data is influenced by factors such as atmospheric conditions, surface emissivity, and the time of image capture, all of which can affect accuracy. Additionally, land surface temperature does not directly represent air temperature, which is often more relevant for human experience. These limitations highlight the need to carefully interpret thermal outputs and understand what is actually being measured when applying these techniques in real-world contexts.

Applications

Thermal remote sensing plays an important role in understanding and managing urban environments, particularly in relation to heat and climate. By deriving land surface temperature from satellite imagery, it is possible to identify spatial patterns of heat across cities and highlight areas that may be more vulnerable to extreme temperatures. This has become increasingly important in the context of climate change, where urban heat is recognised as a growing risk to public health and infrastructure.

One key application is the analysis of the urban heat island effect, where built-up areas experience higher temperatures than surrounding rural regions due to the concentration of heat-retaining materials such as concrete and asphalt. Remote sensing provides a consistent and scalable way to map these patterns, supporting interventions such as increasing urban greening, improving surface materials, or targeting areas most at risk. Studies have shown that satellite-derived land surface temperature can be effectively used to assess urban heat patterns and inform mitigation strategies (e.g. Voogt & Oke (2003)).

These approaches also have practical applications within planning and policy contexts. For example, temperature data can be integrated into spatial analysis to support decisions around urban design, green infrastructure, and climate resilience. When combined with other datasets, such as land use or vegetation cover, thermal data can help to identify priority areas for intervention and support evidence-based policy development. This highlights the value of remote sensing not just as an analytical tool, but as a way to inform real-world decision-making in response to environmental challenges.

However, the application of thermal remote sensing must be approached carefully. Land surface temperature represents the temperature of surfaces rather than the air, meaning it may not directly reflect human thermal experience. Additionally, factors such as time of data capture and surface characteristics can influence results. This reinforces the importance of understanding the limitations of the data when applying it within policy and planning contexts.

Reflection

This week shifted the focus from classifying land cover to understanding physical properties of the Earth’s surface, which felt like a significant step forward in terms of real-world application. Rather than assigning categories, deriving land surface temperature provided a way to quantify environmental conditions, making the outputs feel more directly relevant to current challenges such as climate change and urban heat.

What stood out most was how clearly temperature patterns reflect the structure of the urban environment. Seeing higher temperatures associated with built-up areas and lower temperatures linked to vegetation reinforced the importance of green infrastructure in managing urban heat. It also highlighted how remote sensing can provide evidence to support interventions, rather than relying solely on assumptions about how cities behave.

From a professional perspective, this has clear relevance to work within a London borough, particularly in relation to climate resilience and planning. There is potential to use thermal data to better understand where heat is concentrated and to support initiatives aimed at reducing urban heat impacts, such as increasing tree cover or improving public spaces. This links closely to wider climate and sustainability work, where having spatial evidence can strengthen decision-making and help target interventions more effectively.

However, this week also reinforced the importance of understanding what the data is actually showing. The distinction between land surface temperature and air temperature is particularly important, as it would be easy to misinterpret the results without this awareness. This emphasised that while remote sensing provides powerful insights, it must be used carefully and with a clear understanding of its limitations.

Overall, this week highlighted how remote sensing can move beyond mapping and classification to provide meaningful environmental insights. As my understanding develops, I can see clear opportunities to apply these techniques within a local government context, particularly in supporting climate-related work, while also recognising the importance of interpreting the outputs responsibly.
