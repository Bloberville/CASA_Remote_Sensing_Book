6  Classification

6.1 Weekly Learning Diary

Summary

This week introduced classification techniques in remote sensing, focusing on how satellite imagery can be converted into meaningful categories such as land cover types. Rather than working directly with raw imagery, classification enables pixels to be grouped based on their spectral characteristics, allowing for more interpretable outputs that can be used in analysis and decision-making.

Key concepts included the distinction between supervised and unsupervised classification. Supervised classification involves training the model using known sample data, allowing it to assign classes based on predefined categories, while unsupervised classification groups pixels based on natural patterns within the data without prior labelling. These approaches highlight different ways of interpreting imagery, each with its own advantages and limitations depending on the context and available data.

The practical explored these methods through applying classification techniques to satellite imagery, including defining training areas and generating classified outputs. This demonstrated how the choice of training data and classification method directly influences the final result, reinforcing the importance of careful input selection.

However, several limitations were evident. Classification results can be highly sensitive to the quality and representativeness of training data, and different methods may produce varying outputs for the same dataset. In addition, there is an inherent level of subjectivity in defining classes and training samples, which can introduce bias into the results. These limitations highlight the importance of critically evaluating classification outputs rather than assuming they are inherently accurate.

Applications

Classification techniques are widely used to translate remote sensing imagery into structured and interpretable outputs that can support analysis and decision-making. By assigning pixels to defined categories, such as land cover types, classification enables large volumes of imagery to be summarised in a way that is more accessible and usable within wider analytical workflows.

One of the most common applications of classification is in land cover mapping, where satellite imagery is used to identify and monitor changes in urban areas, vegetation, and water bodies. Supervised classification methods, in particular, are frequently used where training data is available, allowing models to be tailored to specific contexts and objectives. This approach has been widely applied in environmental monitoring and urban studies, where classification outputs provide a consistent way to assess spatial patterns and change over time (e.g. Foody (2002)).

Classification is also increasingly used in automated and large-scale analysis, particularly with the integration of machine learning approaches. These methods allow for more complex patterns within the data to be identified, improving classification accuracy when compared to more traditional techniques. For example, machine learning-based classification has been shown to improve the extraction of land cover information from remotely sensed data, particularly when dealing with heterogeneous or complex environments (e.g. Belgiu & Drăguţ (2016)).

However, the effectiveness of classification in these applications depends heavily on the quality of input data and the selection of training samples. In urban environments like London in particular, classification can be more challenging due to the relative homogeneity of materials, where surfaces such as roads, rooftops, and paved areas often exhibit similar spectral characteristics. This can make it difficult to distinguish between classes that are visually and spectrally alike, increasing the likelihood of misclassification. As a result, higher resolution data, additional spectral bands, or more advanced classification approaches are often required to improve accuracy in city-based contexts. Poorly defined training data or inappropriate classification methods can therefore lead to inaccurate or misleading outputs, which can affect downstream analysis and decision-making. This highlights the importance of combining technical methods with contextual understanding to ensure that classification results are both accurate and meaningful in real-world applications.

Reflection

This week highlighted that while classification can appear straightforward in theory, applying it in practice is far more nuanced. The process of defining training data and selecting appropriate classes made it clear that results are heavily influenced by the decisions made at the outset. Small changes in training samples or class definitions can lead to noticeably different outputs, which reinforced that classification is not purely objective, but requires judgement and iteration.

What stood out most was how challenging classification becomes in urban environments. Unlike more distinct natural landscapes, cities contain a mix of materials that often appear very similar in remotely sensed data. This makes it difficult to clearly separate classes such as different types of built surfaces, and highlights the limitations of relying solely on spectral information. It also emphasised the importance of understanding the local context when interpreting results, rather than taking outputs at face value.

From a professional perspective, this has clear relevance to my role working in a London borough. There is potential to use classification techniques to support areas such as monitoring land use, identifying changes in urban form, or supporting policy objectives linked to the London Plan. It could also support collaboration with colleagues such as ecologists and climate teams, for example in identifying green space, monitoring vegetation change, or providing supporting evidence within work such as Strategic Flood Risk Assessments (SFRA). However, this would require careful consideration of how classes are defined to align with planning and environmental categories, as well as validation to ensure outputs are reliable enough to inform decision-making.

Looking ahead, as my understanding of remote sensing develops, there is an opportunity to explore how classification could be integrated into existing workflows to provide additional spatial insight. Given that this area is not yet widely used within my organisation, there is also potential to shape how it is introduced and applied. This makes it important to develop not only the technical skills required, but also an understanding of how to translate classification outputs into information that is meaningful and usable within a planning and policy context.
